The National Institute of Health (NIH) has established that age is an independent risk factor for the development of cardiovascular diseases (CVD). In others words, despite the presence (or absence) of other risk factors age contributes to an increase in risk for developing some form of heart disease.

It is of importance to note that risk for CVD development and life expectancy are not be used as synonyms. Evidence suggests that in the absence of other risk factors longevity is significantly increased.

In conclusion, even though one can not stop the chronological declines observed in human physiology one can attenuate its impacts by having physically active, nutritionally balanced, and stress-coping habits.


Blood Pressure

Overview

The approach to treat and prevent hypertension is multi-factorial in nature and may include changes to your diet, stress, and physical activity. This is not to say that all must be modified at the same time; changes in any of these categories, independently, haven shown to be beneficial in reversing the rise in high blood pressure in symptomatic individuals.

Nutrition

The recommend approach for those with hypertension is to follow the Dietary Approach for Stopping Hypertension (DASH) guidelines, which is supported by trusted organization such as the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLB) and the Mayo clinic. The hallmark of this action plan is to focus on consumption of vegetables, fruits and low-fat dairy foods; along with moderate amounts of whole grains, fish, poultry and nuts. Due to the close relationship between Sodium intake and blood pressure a modified DASH does exist that emphasizes reduction of this mineral from your food.

Stress Management

Evolutionary speaking, stress is a natural human response to life-threatening situations. As the human species evolved, and with it the commodities of modern life, we have stopped concerning ourselves with lions and tigers, and have replaced the stress responses towards more constant threats, such as job responsibilities, financial obligation, timely commutes, etc. In fact, according to the American Psychological Association the three major causes of stress in the U.S. today are money, work and family responsibilities, with 67%, 65% and 54% of Americans reporting experiencing major feelings of stress in those categories, respectively.

In order to control your stress many strategies are available in which are easy and simple to use. They can range from engaging in mind-body activities such as Yoga and meditation, to simply spending time with loved ones and friends. The idea is for you to create an environment in which you mind can be occupied by tasks that are enjoyable to you and take your mind always from threats; not a fan of Yoga or a pint with friends? Find a new hobby you can enjoy doing.

Physcial Activity

The minimum recommendation for physical activity of 150 minutes a week of moderate aerobic activity per week was not set arbitrarily. This, according to numerous research papers, is the amount of time necessary for the body to respond to the effects of exercise in a positive way. The benefits are too many to list here, but direct to blood pressure they include: improved heart function, reduction in resting heart rate, increase vascularization of muscle, and enhanced ability to manage stress (and its hormones) – all in all an very effective way to reduce blood pressure.


Stress

Overview

Evolutionary speaking, stress is a natural human response to life-threatening situations. As the human species evolved, and with it the commodities of modern life, we have stopped concerning ourselves with lions and tigers, and have replaced the stress responses towards more constant threats, such as job responsibilities, financial obligation, timely commutes, etc. In fact, according to the American Psychological Association the three major causes of stress in the U.S. today are money, work and family responsibilities, with 67%, 65% and 54% of Americans reporting experiencing major feelings of stress in those categories, respectively.

A College Student Health Survey conducted in Minnesota by Boynton Health showed that the amount of stress student have may have less devastating impacts to one’s health if they are well suited to appropriately cope with them. In other words, it may of higher value to learn to cope with current stressors rather than trying to eliminate or (even worse) neglect their existence. Moreover, it may be that these same findings extend outside the confines of campuses and are true for working adults as well.

In order to better cope with stress it is first essential that its sources can be identified. Once identified, one must be able to determine his/her locust of control over it; can it be controllable? Here is an example, and a practical exercise you can employ:

“You are about to fall asleep when you remember that you forgot to send an important work document to your boss. You toss and turn thinking about what will happen, you can even get up and send a panicked-hurried email to your boss to alleviate the situation. However, before you do all of the above think about the following questions: will sending this email, right now, change anything? Even if you do send the email at this time, will the recipient even see it?

You may feel better about sending the email, but in reality, the boss will not even see it until the start of the workday. Or, even worse, let’s say that they do see it! Now you have stressed your boss at a late time of the night; think they will be happier to see the email before they went to bed? Or in the morning, when they should actually be at work?”

The example above can be used to many situations, banks for example do not complete transfers at night (bills paid at midnight won’t be posted until the next day’s work hours). The point here being that the past cannot be changed, no matter how hard you think of it; and the actions taken at the present moment may not always fix the problem. Now, if the answers to the questions is that your actions will change things, then go ahead, pull up those sleeves and get it done! If not, you are creating a life-threatening situation in your brain, and the body will respond accordingly.

Controlling stress is about recognizing your role in engaging with the stress response in the first place. Additionally, try to find activities that you enjoy doing outside of work and other obligations. If you are feeling stressed and down, typically people ask “what is the matter with me”. Better yet, is asking your yourself “what matters to me”? This simple reframing can put things into perspective of where to better focus your energy and efforts.

In a nutshell, there are many things one can do to control stress, but to say that there is one single solution to combat stress is fictional. Stress, and its subsequent control, is highly individual. Identify the sources of your stress, label your efforts and actions against realistic outcomes, and choose to address the things that are high on your “this matter to me” list.


Physical Inactivity

Metric Value Points Earned Risk Level
Physical Inactivity: {{data.inactivity_value}} {{data.inactivity_score}}/12 {{vm.inactivityRisk}}

Overview

Movement is required for you to exercise; however, to equate movement with exercise is erroneous and perhaps one of the barriers why so many of us are sedentary. Mathematically speaking: Movement ≠ Exercise, but exercise completion is dependent on how much you move.

Most people are taught that the shortest way to health is to “eat less, exercise more”, so far… this is not working so great as, simply put, people are not doing it. What should be told, and is far more realistic, is to “eat the right foods, and move more”.

Breaking up the amount of time of inactivity in your day has been demonstrated to improve your health, including alteration to mood and blood values. Research indicates that the detrimental impacts of physical inactivity happen when we sit for more than 2 hours at a time. Henceforth, be more cognizant of your sitting time (or even set an timer on your phone) in which you must stand up for at least 5 min (10 is preferred) every 2 hours.

This does not necessarily mean you are losing 10 minutes of productivity of your day, I fact data shows this little physical break also brings along positive impacts to your brain. Instead of sending an email to a co-worker that is down the hall, go see them. Need a bathroom break? How about some water? All of these things can help you boost your productivity at work while combating common ailments to your overall health.

Even at home is easy to add more movement to your day. Binge watching TV series has becomes America’s new past time, and without commercial interruption during and in between shows, people no longer see the need to get up off the couch. Results from recent survey indicates that Americans spent (on average) 5 hours sitting in front of the TV. The same rules apply here as they did in the office: stand up between each episode (if an hour long) or two episodes (if your traditional comedy) and take a bathroom break, refill your water, and/or get a snack (chose the right ones). The important thing is not too sit for too long without fighting gravity, give your body little doses of gravitational reality and your health will thank you.

Waist Size

Metric Value Points Earned Risk Level
Waist Size (in.): {{data.waist_value}} {{data.waist_score}}/10 {{vm.waistRisk}}

Overview

Current research indicates waist obesity to be a major health concern, not necessary directly to the amount of fat one contains, but because is an indication of systemic inflammation. This does not mean you have a fever or are sick, it means that the body is constantly fighting stressors and poor nutrients and this can have severe negative outcomes to your health.

Nutrition

In order to control waist size one must pay attention to their nutrition and stress (of course physical activity expedites health improvements). Nutritionally speaking, seek foods that are anti-inflammatory such as foods containing probiotics and anti- oxidants (examples include fermented foods and red fruits, respectively). These small additions will give your body the tools to properly reduce inflammation; at the same time, reduce consumption of foods containing added sugar, preservatives, and processed oils as they have been labeled pro-inflammatory.

Stress Response

During stressful times, the body releases a hormone that triggers the use of sugars in your blood. It may seem as good thing at first, but the constant activation of stress will leave you depleted of sugar in the blood and exhausted; in other words you are tired and hangry. Typically, people in stressful times don’t tend to choose healthy foods as an option, not because they don’t know the difference between right or wrong, but driven from a physiological need to replace energy sources. Minimize the stress in your life by avoiding situations that trigger this physiological response - that will help you curb the need for constant sugar and push you away from that midday crash.


Family History

Metric Value Points Earned Risk Level
Family History of CVDs: {{vm.historyDisplay}} {{data.family_history_score}}/5 {{vm.historyRisk}}
In a recent study published by the College of Family Physicians of Canada, evidence for development of cardiovascular diseases (CVD) based on family history is strong in the following circumstances. The offspring of parents with premature CVD have a 60% to 75% risk increase for CVD development. Additionally, siblings of patients with CVD have about a 40% risk increase.

These findings are in agreement in numerous previous studies demonstrating similar strengths in this relationship between CVD and family history. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) provides in its website similar information pertaining to this topic.

Important to note that although family history, and hence the genes received for your parents, are not modifiable it does not establish certainty of disease development. Habitual acts of physical activity, proper nutrition, and stress management are key in moderating genes and preventing likelihood of disease development.


Physical Activity

Metric Value Points Earned Risk Level
Physical Activity (min/week): {{data.physical_activity_value}} {{data.physical_activity_score}}/16 {{vm.activityRisk}}

Overview

The minimum recommendation for physical activity of 150 minutes a week of moderate aerobic activity per week was not set arbitrarily. This, according to numerous research papers, is the amount of time necessary for the body to respond to the effects of exercise in a positive way. The benefits are too many to list here, but direct include: improved heart function, reduction in resting heart rate, increase vascularization of muscle, enhanced ability to manage stress (and its hormones), and improved cognition (mental functioning).

For some individuals the idea of 150 minutes per week may be overwhelming, but once this number is broken in more tangible portions it becomes something attainable and realistic for most. First, these 150 minutes can be broken down into individual sessions of 30 minutes each; therefore as long as it is done 5x week this guideline can be satisfied.

Additionally, if 30 minutes still seems to be too much of a jump from your current routine the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) states that the same cardiorespiratory benefits from physical activity can be attained with 3 individual session of 10 minutes each (for a total of 30 minutes in one day). Start with small goals like 10 minutes in the morning, afternoon (after lunch is ideal), and evening. As you become more habituated with exercise you can start increasing the duration of your sessions.

At an even broader scale, and taking into consideration a day has 1,440 minutes and a week has 10,080 minutes, the guidelines are only asking for a 0.015% of your time each week for physical activity. So much for the “I don’t have time” excuse…

Sleep

Metric Value Points Earned Risk Level
Sleep (hrs/night): {{data.sleep_value}} {{data.sleep_score}}/10 {{vm.sleepRisk}}

Overview

Here are some tips that may help you sleep more soundly and wake up feeling more refreshed. You might want to start slowly at first. Pick one thing to change, and see how that change affects your sleep. After a week or two, try to add another change. As you make changes, you might want to use a sleep journal(What is a PDF document?) to figure out what things help you to sleep better and what things may get in the way of a good night's sleep. Step by step, your sleep should improve. If it doesn't, talk to your doctor.

Nutrition

  1. Limit caffeine (coffee, tea, caffeinated sodas) during the day, and don't have any for at least 4 to 6 hours before bedtime.
  2. Do not drink alcohol late in the evening. You may fall asleep with no problems, but drinking alcohol before bed can wake you up later in the night. Otherwise, drink in moderation. Try to limit alcohol to 2 standard drinks camera.gif a day for men and 1 drink a day for women.
  3. Avoid heavy meals close to bedtime. But a light snack may help you sleep.
  4. Don't go to bed thirsty. But don't drink so much that you have to get up often to urinate during the night.

Healthy Habits to Improve Sleep

  1. Go to bed at a regular bedtime every night.
  2. Wake up at the same time each day, including weekends, even if you haven't slept well.
  3. Get regular exercise. Don't exercise within 3 to 4 hours of bedtime, because the activity can make it hard to get to sleep.
  4. Try to not use technology such as smartphones, computers, or tablet devices in the hours before bed. The light from these devices and the emotions that can result from checking email or social media sites can make it harder to unwind and fall asleep.
  5. Do something relaxing before bedtime. Try deep breathing, yoga, meditation, tai chi, or muscle relaxation. Take a warm bath. Play a quiet game, or read a book.


Body Mass Index (BMI)

Metric Value Risk Level Chart
Body Mass Index (BMI): {{vm.mybmi}} {{vm.bmiRisk}}
BMI is not part of the Wellness Score and is calculated as a result of the collected height and weight metrics within this application. BMI is not an independent risk factor of CVDs as the calculation is primarily used to aggregate population data and has limitations when applied to individual persons. BMI does not distinguish between weight associated with muscle and weight associated with fat and thus should be analyzed in combination with waist measurement which is, by itself, an independent risk factor for CVDs. There is strong evidence linking obesity to CVDs and while BMI is not the best tool for analyzing the body composition at the individual level, it does provide a way to aggregate weight against height and when combined with waist size, can indicate signs of obesity and factors contributing to metabolic syndrome. Generally, a BMI > 30 would be considered a high value and can indicate obesity. However, a moderate to high BMI, but an optimal waist size would still indicate optimal risk as waist size is the independent risk factor for CVDs.
