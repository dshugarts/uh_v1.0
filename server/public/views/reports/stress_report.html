According to the American Psychological Association the three major causes of stress in the U.S. today are money, work and family responsibilities, with 67%, 65% and 54% of Americans reporting experiencing major feelings of stress in those categories, respectively. Adults report stress to cause negative impacts on both their physical and mental health; yet, the majority feel as if they do not do enough to proactively cope with it.

The impacts of stress, if left unchecked, can lead to mental health diagnosis such as depression, anxiety, and other mood disorders as well as changes in biometrics such as increases in weight and/or blood pressure. Stress is also a well-known contributor to the development of metabolic disease (i.e.: diabetes) and cardiovascular diseases.

For example, the over reliance on the stress response is harmful in many ways and has a direct impact on your cardiovascular system. The stress response causes a phenomenon in your arteries known as vasoconstriction - the arteries of your body narrow in diameter in other to facilitate blood flow to muscles and tissues. Each individual stress response elevates blood pressure. With each, and more stressful, events leading to higher levels of Systolic and Diastolic pressures. In other words, stress has a significant and powerful impact on blood pressure.

In its recent report on stress the APA also showcases alarming data that the younger section of our population demonstrates the highest rate of “poor mental” health. However, as pointed out by the Boynton Health’s College Student Health Survey, the amount of stress one has may have less devastating impacts to one’s health if they are well suited to appropriately cope with them. In other words, it may be more about learning to cope with current stressors rather than eliminating or (even worse) neglecting its existence.
